Designing for Failure

On designing resilient systems that fail gracefully and recover quickly when things go wrong.

Expect components to fail—network links break, disks fill, and third-party services misbehave. Designing for failure means identifying critical paths and ensuring they degrade gracefully when dependencies are unavailable.

Implement patterns like circuit breakers, timeouts, and bulkheads to isolate faults. Provide sensible defaults for retries and backoff to avoid amplifying failure modes. Where possible, return cached or partial results rather than failing hard.

Recovery automation is crucial: runbooks, health checks, and automatic restarts reduce mean-time-to-recovery. However, keep operators informed with clear signals and escalation paths so human judgment can intervene when automation reaches its limits.

Regular chaos exercises and tabletop drills help validate assumptions and uncover brittle components. Make learning from incidents a routine part of the engineering process by documenting findings and teaching them back to the team.
